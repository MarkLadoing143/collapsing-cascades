Background

What is Collapsing Cascades?

Collapsing Cascades is a fictional series about what were to happen if a major earthquake were to hit North America. While the story itself is complete fictitious, the setting in which it takes places and the events that surround it are in fact based on reality.

Development History

Development began shortly after the Great East Japan Earthquake on March 11, 2011, which coincidently was also my 16th Birthday. Distraught and missing to memorialise the events, I began creating Collapsing Cascades as a tale of survival, comradely, community, and bravery as the citizens of North America work together to make it through adversity. In such a time of divisiveness, unhappiness, and hatred, I written Collapsing Cascades to reflect the bad and good of tragdey and how the human spirit can fight on in such darkness.

I also wrote Collapsing Cascades to be the antithesis of what I saw in many American disaster films and media. More often or not, disaster films focus more on the destruction and chaos, with characters becoming nothing more than objects to run from this destruction. This generally creates narratives that are unrealisitc and characters that are bland, unlikable one-note tropes with little development or growth. My goal with Collapsing Cascades was to create a human-centered piece where the aftermath of the disaster is depicted, with people struggling to gather themselves up and work together, while the world around them literally crumbled.

Recently, Collapsing Cascades received some major updates in its setting and characters, as I felt that the premise did not fit what I was trying to write. In its original form, the story was set in Seattle, Washington, with two main characters being of Northern European decent. Oliver was named Oliver Knutson Lindberg and was Swedish American, Tim was named Timothy Koski Heikinen and was Finnish Canadian, and both had blue eyes and blond hair. After some time, I became unhappy with this approach as their characterizations felt static and unrealistic, and thus completely overhaul the story. I moved it to Vancouver as I was more familiar with the area, changing their nationalities in the process to better reflect the ethnic demographics of the city, which happens to be predominantly Chinese Canadian. I also wanted to portray a demographic which I felt was under-represented in Canadian media and show off the multicultural side of Canada, and how we as a country can work together to get through anything, even tragedy.

The Science of Collapsing Cascades

Surrounding the coast of the Pacific Northwest is the Cascadia Subduction Zone, an area where the Juan de Fuca tectonic plate converges with the North American Plate. Currently, the Juan de Fuca pushes underneath the North American, causing much of the volcanic activity that occurs the area. But should the Juan de Fuca Plate become "locked" (ie. become trapped) under the North American Plate, the pressure and force built up could cause a sudden rupture, triggering a megathrust earthquake similar to that which occurred with the 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami and the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake. Experts have determined that a megathrust earthquake did occur some 300 years ago, causing a tsunami that proportedly reached Eastern Japan, and that their is a 37% chance of a magnitude 8.0 or higher earthquake occuring within the next 50 years.

Inspirations

Collapsing Cascades' tone and story were inspired by two pieces of media.

The first was a Japanese Anime series called Tokyo Magnitude 8.0. Aired in 2009, the 11-episode series tells the story of two Japanese children, Mirai and Yuki Onozawa, and their attempt to get home after a magnitude 8.0 hits the center of Tokyo. Unlike most disaster stories, Tokyo Magnitude 8.0 is very downturn and at times somber, with the kids often struggling with feelings of stress and sadness due to the destruction and the thought of their families being "gone". It was a series which I felt really reflected what I wanted Collapsing Cascades to be like.

The other is a English-Language Spanish film called The Impossible. Released in 2012, the film is based on the true story of a Spanish family that survived the 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami during a vacation in Thailand. Much like Tokyo Magnitude 8.0, the film mostly centers on the family's attempt to survive, as they are seperated by the tsunami and must find one another, despite evidence to say the other is long gone and probably dead.
